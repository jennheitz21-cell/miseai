A letter from your proprietors

Who's behind the pass

The House Paper · Special EditionEst. on 50 years of shifts

Between the two of us, we've put fifty years into this industry — forty of them at the management level. Jenn's side of the house is the bar: fifteen years running bar programs, everything from high-traffic rooms like the Oakland Arena down to small houses built on the craft — mixology-driven lists, even a 31-tap system that changed weekly. Then five more years managing full service. Mikey's side is the kitchen: started at fifteen in the dish pit and worked up the line the long way, executive chef for the last fifteen years, and for eight of those an owner — a food truck and a restaurant, gastro-pub style, farm-to-table.

We didn't learn this business from a dashboard. We learned it from the people who feed the Bay Area — Marin Roots, Capay, Full Belly Farms; early-morning runs to the Marin and San Francisco Civic Center markets; and every purveyor you've ever taken a 6 AM delivery from: Sysco, US Foods, BiRite, Performance, GreenLeaf and the small guys, Del Monte Meats, Golden Gate Meat Company, Royal Hawaiian Seafood, Monterey Fish, Ports Seafood. We've run the systems too — Toast, Upserve, Aloha, OpenTable, every to-go platform that promised to save us. We know exactly what they cost and exactly what they don't do.

Here's the honest reason this exists. The spreadsheets stopped working. There was a time you could eyeball your costs — you just knew. Since COVID, prices move weekly, sometimes overnight. A fifty-cent bump on one item used to be easy to catch; now everything moves too fast for anybody's eyeball.

And when Mikey owned and operated, there was no time in the day for a data crunch. The day started at 5 AM — shower, coffee in the dark, figuring out the market list on the drive to Oakland, then US Foods, then Jetro in Concord, back over the bridge, ready for service at 11. A couple of cooks and a night dish guy. We split the dishwashing; his wife washed and expedited and covered shifts. He covered his cooks' breaks day and night. End of the day was when you found out where you stood — and it wasn't always easy to look at.

This system is not for restaurants that need to know where they stand every minute of the day. That was never us — we were cooking. It's for knowing where you stand at the end of a shift, or on the drive to the market the next morning, with enough time to make a few tweaks and get the maximum out of your dollar.

It's for the owner who wears every hat — running the kitchen, watching the dining room, placing the orders. It's for the house that has a chef and a manager and an owner, each of whom needs their own board. It's for the solo chef who just wants to know where the month landed. Honestly? It's for everybody who cooks for a living.

Why is it so much cheaper than the competition? Simple: we didn't spend millions on data research, and there's no big company behind us to feed. There's fifty years of combined experience behind this build, and a stubborn rule — no manual entry for the operator or the chef. We've used the systems where it felt like we were doing all the work for them. That never made sense to us.

So every tier costs less than a small cup of coffee a day, delivered the way a chef's day actually runs. And here's our promise in writing: the price you sign up at is your price — it will never go up. As AI gets cheaper, your bill gets lower. No hidden charges. Nothing but simple. For the chef, the system automates roughly three-quarters of the admin work — the office hours California law makes you count, the hours that pull you off the line right when mistakes get expensive. Get that time back. Spend it on the food, or on the weekly inventory that actually deserves your energy.

One last thing, because it's really the whole thing. Mikey grew up in San Bernardino County — nobody's heard of it, that's fine. Down the street was a little French kitchen, not in the best part of the neighborhood. His mom threw his birthday parties there — yes, snails for the first course, at a kid's birthday. His dad sold wine to the owner and ran kitchens himself, so the family ate there often, and every time, the chef would come out from the back, walk to the table smiling, and talk with them — and you were so proud that he came to your table. The way he spoke about everything stuck forever.

Places like that are closing, and we don't like the number. This project is for them. Something simple, something honest — so the person who comes out from the back to your table gets to keep doing it.

Chef Mikey & Jenn
