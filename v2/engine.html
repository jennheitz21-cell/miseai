The Engine Room

How it works, told plain

No tech lecture. Just what you bring on day one, and what the crew in the back does with it — chef to chef.

The trade

Simple as a purveyor order: here's what you send us, and here's what comes back. No manual entry, ever — that's the house rule.

What You Send

  1. Your recipes — current menu only, with current pricing. Pars included if you keep them. Don't keep pars? Send us three months of product mix instead and we'll build your pars for you.
  2. Your menu as it runs today, with pricing.
  3. Your schedule — first name and a last initial, plus the hourly rate. That's all. No tax paperwork, no socials, no life stories. We know the California labor tax law — and every other state's — so we simply add the right burden percentage to the total. Your true labor cost, nearly exact, without handing anyone's private information to a company you just met. We wouldn't do it with our people either.
  4. Your prep lists.
  5. Your order sheets — with accurate pricing.
  6. A current inventory list — what you're actually running, not last year's.
  7. Your purveyor list — could be Sysco, could be Costco. No judgment, it all counts the same.
  8. What kind of house you run — sandwich shop, food truck, coffee shop, juice bar, full bar with no food. It all lives under one umbrella here. Yes, even a coffee shop — the system tracks cost down to the to-go cup and builds your recipes-of-record for you. Prepacked goods are fine too.

What Comes Back

  1. Your recipe book, costed to the penny — every plate, every pour, wired to your live prices so the costs move when your invoices move.
  2. Your pars, built and breathing — set from your real usage, adjusting with your seasons automatically.
  3. Your invoices, read every night — every line, every cent flagged, purveyors compared against each other.
  4. Your boards, populated before dawn — cook, chef, manager, owner, each seeing their own station.
  5. The Morning Brief — one honest page about yesterday, waiting when you wake up.
  6. Your true labor cost — burden included, no paperwork collected.

Who this house serves: independents under $1 million a year in sales. Solo bars. Food trucks. The solo chef who just wants to know where the month landed. The small café with a beer menu. The gastro pub with a dozen kegs on the list. The full bar with no kitchen at all. If you're the one wearing the hats, this was built for you.

Pars that move with you

Here's what the product mix buys you. Your numbers run through BigQuery — Google's data engine, the same machinery the big corporate groups run their whole operations on. The last couple of years it's gotten dramatically better and cheaper, and that's a real part of why our menu is priced in cents.

What it does for your kitchen: it builds pars for the chef. If you already keep your own pars, great — you know the value. The difference is these pars change over time. Run the same menu year-round and the system learns your peaks and your slow seasons, and quietly adjusts your par levels based on what you actually use. No more prepping for July in January.

The invoice sniffer

His whole job is your invoices. Every invoice sent to the house goes through him first — he cleans it, organizes it, gets it ready for the books, because the engine only takes clean data, no mess. By the time you're awake, the prep work's done.

He flags everything — even a cent over. And you'll know about it in the morning. Running multiple purveyors like we did — Sysco and US Foods both, say? He compares them automatically and flags when the same product is cheaper across the street.

And he's smart enough to know a prime steak isn't a commodity steak, and an organic tomato isn't the one that rode up from Mexico. We're not comparing apples and oranges — that's the whole point.

The overnight brigade

Why does the heavy work happen in the middle of the night? Because that's when AI is cheapest to run — everybody's asleep, the batch lanes are wide open, and the daily free allowances on the fast models go a long way. It makes a huge difference in the big picture, and it's a big piece of why every tier costs less than a small coffee. The AI lives inside the data engine, right next to your numbers — your data never gets shipped out somewhere to be read; the questions come to it. That keeps it fast, private, and cheap.

The fast one · works all night

The Prep Cook

Cheapest and fastest on the line. He parses every invoice that came in that day — every line, every price — cleans it and stages it for the books. He helps with inventory deduction and the prep lists, and he's the one behind the Spanish toggle on the cook dashboard: the translations are stored ready to go, so when a cook hits the switch it flips in a fraction of a second.

The sharp one · closes the night

The Sous

Smarter, still quick. He checks the Prep Cook's work before anything counts, and when the Prep Cook's day is done, the Sous finishes the shift. Then he goes to the books and pulls everything needed to populate every board you'll see in the morning — leaving only one job undone: the story.

The one who writes · before dawn

The Exec

The most capable of the crew. He reads the clean, finished numbers — and before he writes a word, he does his own homework: fact-checks, digs, verifies. Then he writes your Morning Brief. That's why the House Paper reads true.

Your assistant · 15 minutes a day · chef's board

The Chef's Assistant

A different breed — this one's yours, live, during the day. One AI running all day is expensive, so we did it on purpose: treat the AI like an employee. Come in ready. "Today we're costing." He reads a month of your books before he even clocks in — walks in knowing more than the chef, the owner, and the accountant combined. Read him your recipes and he builds every recipe card with the cost attached — work that used to eat a chef's whole afternoon. Menu change? Done with your eyes closed. Or push him: "What needs to move off my menu, and build me a special at 24% food cost" — and you're reading ten options in seconds.

That's the brigade. Nobody on it takes a salary, nobody calls in sick, and the whole crew costs you less than a soda a day — because we run the most current AI the smart way instead of the expensive way, and Google's pricing on this machinery genuinely works in the little guy's favor. We pass that straight through.
